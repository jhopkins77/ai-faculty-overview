Again, it might act upon other things besides number, were objects found whose mutual fundamental relations could be expressed by those of the abstract science of operations, and which should be also susceptible of adaptations to the action of the operating notation and mechanism of the engine. Supposing, for instance, that the fundamental relations of pitched sounds in the science of harmony and of musical composition were susceptible of such expression and adaptations, the engine might compose elaborate and scientific pieces of music of any degree of complexity or extent.

Examples of simple neural nets

Large Language Models

Large Language Models use HUGE numbers of CPU's to take the relationship between words and concepts in the English language and turn them into numbers

Those numbers come from anylyzing pretty much all the languge that's publicly availble (e.g., Internet posts)

Those numbers can be then used to create answers to new combinations of words in response to certain stimuli (e.g., questions from users)

Here's (an insanely simplified version of)what that looks like

Here's how an LLM can then respond to questions

Input: What caused the fall of the Roman Empire?

In this way, an LLM can creates content that's the most likely response, based on its data, to any given prompt

Widely-used LLM's (e.g., ChatGPT) all operate on this principal. There are still differences between models:

  • How much data do they have access to?
  • The number of CPUs they have available (more CPU's = more complex neural nets that can do more things)
  • How much "fine-tuning" (tweaking of weights based on new data and situation-specific commands) they've done

Ultimately though, neural nets remain the foundational concept of all LLM's.

This is true even when you're dealing with personal content, aka "Retreval-augmented generation" (RAG)

RAG's just take your submitted documents and layer them on top of the answer the LLM otherwise would produce.

What types of things do you think LLM's will do well?

Some ideas

  • Things that are generic
  • Things that have rigid formats and conventions (e.g, coding)
  • Things that only need to be "good enough"
  • Things for which there is already a lot of writing
  • Things for which there are clear answers
  • Things that are lower stakes

What are some things that LLM's can't do well with?

  • Things that are novel
  • Things that have fluid formats and conventions (e.g, creative writing)
  • Things that need to be very good.
  • Things for which there isn't a lot of writing
  • Things for which there aren't clear answers
  • Things that are higher stakes.

Ethical Questions

1. Hallucination & Bias

As we've discussed, AI is always looking to produce the most likely answer to a response.

This means it's not always considering whether words are reflecting accurate or impartial information

As is a result, it can sometimes insert innacurrate or biased info (e.g., inserting sources that don't actually exist)

2. Copyright

There is a live debate on whether AI systems have violated copright in training their models on Internet data.

Companies like OpenAI have been sued by content producers, who say they should be compensated for their intellectual property being incorproated into LLM's.

If all this makes you feel generally uncomfortable or if particular AI content feels too close to human-generated works, it may be worht avoiding AI.

3. Environmental Issues

AI systems rely on large data centes that use huge amounts of power. This is already having an impact on local power grids

While individal AI prompts are not energy intensive, large sums or prompts are.

Energy companies aren't always responding to these dmeand surges with renewable energy, which you may want to consider when using them.

How to incorporate this into your teaching?

There are two big places where you need to think about AI and how it impacts your work

  • The curricular materials you produce
  • The materials you ask students to produce

Let's look at that first item

Think back to the types of tasks AI is good at:

  • Things that are generic
  • Things with rigid conventions
  • Things that just need to "good enough"
  • Things for which there is already a lot of writing
  • Things for which there are clear answers
  • Things that are lower stakes

What things in your teaching meet these criteria?

There are several places where it is appopriate for you to use AI

  • Syllabus planning
  • Lesson planning
  • Slide creation
  • Generating use cases and hypotheticals
  • Assignments and assignment descriptions
  • Summarizing lectures
  • Locating sources
  • Summarizing your own work

There are also situation where AI could be appropriate, but it's debatable

  • Grading
  • Personalized feedback
  • Communication with students
  • Summarizing others' work

How to know which situations are appropriate and which are not?

Two professors, Prof Rick Dakan (Ringling College of Art and Design) and Prof Joseph Feller (University College Cork), developed The AI Fluency Framework.

This a framework I'd recommend you keep in mind when using AI

One warning: however you use AI, be prepared to:

Disclose when you're using it.

Informatively explain why you did or did not use AI

Informatively explain why students can or cannot use AI

There are a lot of alarmist headlines about AI use among students

Data presents a more complicated picture

Contra to our worst fears, a lot of students actually care about their education and don't want a computer to just do the work for them.

However, there are situations where student make use of AI

Student turn to AI when they feel that what they're being asked to do isn't important.

This makes sense in light of the things we previously mentioned as being useful places for AI use when producing curricular materials.

Strategies for how to deal with AI

  • High stakes testing
    • Testing in-person
    • Making the test timed
    • Making the exam oral
    • The nuclear option:Blue books
  • Moodle's Safe Exam Browser (SEB)
    • Does require students to download the SEB software to their computer
    • Is one of the more complex features in Moodle
  • Incorporate stuff that's not on the Internet (e.g., sources only available via print).
  • Ask novel questions (e.g., stuff involving current events, recently released data, etc.)
    • Ones involving current events
    • Data that's not publicly available
    • Ones that haven't been written about on the Internet
  • Make AI part of the assignment
    • Submit two drafts (first draft from AI, second from the student who's edited the AI's work)
    • Enter your prompt into AI, show it to students when introducing assignment
    • Have students include chat logs with AI tools as part of their assignment.
